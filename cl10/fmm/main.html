Primary Market

What is primary market?

  • Segments of financial Market also known as new issue market.
  • Market place where companies, governments, or other institutions raise capital by issuing new Securities, such as shares for the first time.
  • The key characteristics of the primary market is that funds flow directly from the investors to the issuer.

What is the role of primary market ?

  • Serves the crucial role of capital formation.
  • Provides a platform for the issuers to raise the necessary funds for various purposes, such as:
  • starting new business
  • expanding and existing business
  • modernising a diversifying operations
  • For investors, it provides in opportunity to purchase security is direct from the issuers.

Face value of share/debenture

  • It is it's nominal value, as assigned by company at the time of issuance.
  • Original value printed on a physical share certificate or stated in the company's record.
  • Unlike market price, face value is a fixed amount that doesn't change with market fluctuations.
  • For a share, the face value is a foundational accounting figure.
  • For a debenture, the face value is the principle amount that the issuer promises to repay the debenture holder at the time of maturity.

Premium and Discount

  • When a security is sold above its face value --> premium.
  • When a security is sold below its face value --> discount.
  • Security guard generally issued in the denomination of 5 ,10, 100.


Different types of issues

  • Public issues: open to general public.
  1. Initial public offering: first time offer of the security by an unlisted company.
  2. Further public offering: additional issue by an already listed company.
  • Right issue: securities offered to an existing shareholders in a specific ratio.
  • Preferential issues: shares issued to a selected group not a public or right issue to quickly raise equity capital.

Share price and market capitalisation

  • Initial price at which a company's shares are offered in the primary market.
  • Calculated by multiplying the current share price by the number of shares in issue.

Public issue Vs Private placement

  • Public issue: open to general public and involves allotment for 50 or more shares.
  • Private placement: limited towards selected group, allotment made to less than 50 persons.


Book building process

  • Mechanism for efficient price discovery during IPOs.
  • Investors bid at or above floor price during the open period.
  • The final issue price is determined after the bid closing date.
  • Allows daily demand tracking compared to single fixed demand of a normal price issue.
  1. Cut off price : final issue price decided after considering all bids.
  2. Floor price: minimum price for the bid.
  3. Price Band: range within which the investors can build with the cap not exceeding 120% of the floor price.

Other concepts

  • Lock in: restrictions on sale of shares for a specified period to ensure promoters retained a stake post issue.
  • Listing of securities: admission of security for trading on a stock exchange.
  • Delisting of securities: permanent removal of security is from the stock exchange.
  • Listing agreement: at the time of listing securities of a company on a stock exchange, the company is required to enter into a listing agreement with the exchange . The agreement specifies the terms and conditions of listing and the disclosure on a continuous basis to the exchange .
  • Prospectus: document containing detailed information about the company and the issue ; used to inform potential investors.
  • Regulators role:
    • Finalizers the list of allottees.
    • Ensure credit to demat account.
    • Dispatches refund order and co-ordinate listing.
  • SEBI's role in primary market
    • Ensures adequate disclosure in the prospectus but does not recommend or guarantee issue.
    • Investors must make informed decisions based on the offer document content.
  • American depository receipt
    • Traded in US dollars.
    • Helps companies raise money from US investors.
    • It allows foreign investors to invest with Indian regulation.
  • Global depository receipt
    • Here shares of an Indian company is traded in market outside the US.
    • Ex: Reliance GDR in London stock exchange.
  • Features of GDR
    • Traded in forcing currencies like Euro/Pound.
    • Provides global recognition for Indian company.
    • Allows foreign investors to buy Indian shares easily.
  • Indian depository receipt
    • Shares of foreign companies traded in India.
    • Ex: Standard Charter Bank in India
